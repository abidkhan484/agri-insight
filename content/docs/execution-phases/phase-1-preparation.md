# Phase 1: Preparation (Week 1–2)

> **Skill Level:** 🟢 Beginner — No prior ZBNF experience needed
> **Time Required:** 10–14 days
> **Goal:** Gather all materials, select your test plot, and stop chemical inputs

---

## 🎯 Phase Objective

Before you apply a single drop of Jeevamrutha, you need to prepare. This phase is about gathering materials, choosing the right plot, and understanding what you're working with. **Skipping this phase is the #1 reason beginners fail.**

---

## Daily Task Breakdown

### Day 1–2: Select Your Test Plot

- [ ] 🟢 Walk your land and choose a **small test plot** (5–10 decimals / 200–400 sq meters)
- [ ] 🟢 Pick a plot you can observe daily (close to home is best)
- [ ] 🟢 Avoid the worst soil on your farm — pick average quality land
- [ ] 🟢 If possible, keep a same-size **control plot** where you continue chemical farming (for comparison later)

**Why small?** If something goes wrong, you lose very little. If it succeeds, you have proof to scale up.

**How to choose:**
- Flat or gently sloped land (avoid waterlogging spots)
- Not under heavy shade (needs 4+ hours of sunlight)
- Has a water source nearby (tube well, canal, or pond)

### Day 3–4: Find Your Desi Cow Source

- [ ] 🟢 Identify a source of **indigenous cow** dung and urine
- [ ] 🟢 Best breeds: Red Chittagong (লাল বিরিশ), Pabna, North Bengal Grey, Mirkadim
- [ ] 🟢 If you own a desi cow → perfect, use your own
- [ ] 🟢 If you don't → visit neighbors, nearby villages, or local cattle markets
- [ ] 🟢 Negotiate a regular supply — you need **10 kg dung + 5–10L urine per batch** (every 15 days)

**⚠️ Critical:** Do NOT use crossbreed (Holstein-Friesian, Sahiwal cross) cow products. They have significantly fewer beneficial microbes. Only desi/indigenous breeds work effectively.

**🟡 Tip:** Collect dung and urine **early morning** when the cow first stands up — freshest and highest microbial count.

### Day 5–7: Gather All Materials

- [ ] 🟢 **Plastic drum (200L)** — Buy or find a used one. Never metal (kills microbes). Check: no chemical residue inside.
- [ ] 🟢 **Gunny bags (2–3)** — For covering the drum during fermentation
- [ ] 🟢 **Wooden stick (long)** — For stirring. Not metal, not plastic.
- [ ] 🟢 **Jaggery / গুড়** — 2 kg. Date palm jaggery (খেজুরের গুড়) or sugarcane molasses (চিটাগুড়)
- [ ] 🟢 **Pulse flour / বেসন** — 2 kg. Masoor dal, mung dal, or chickpea flour. Any local mill can grind this.
- [ ] 🟢 **Lime / চুন** — 50g. Paan-er chun from any paan shop. Very cheap.
- [ ] 🟢 **Fertile soil** — 1 handful from under a banyan tree (বটগাছ), bor tree, or old bamboo grove. This soil is rich in native microbes.

**For pest formulations (collect now, store for later):**
- [ ] 🟢 **Neem leaves** — Collect 10+ kg. Available from roadside neem trees everywhere in Bangladesh.
- [ ] 🟢 **Tobacco** — 500g. Biri tobacco leaves from Rangpur region or local markets.
- [ ] 🟢 **Green chillies** — 500g. From your garden or bazaar.
- [ ] 🟢 **Garlic** — 250g. From bazaar.
- [ ] 🟢 **Cloth bag** — For Beejamrutha preparation. Any old cotton cloth works.

### Day 8–9: Document Your Starting Point

- [ ] 🟢 **Soil color test**: Dig 6 inches in your test plot. Note the color (light brown = poor, dark brown/black = better).
- [ ] 🟢 **Earthworm count**: Dig a 1 sq ft area, 6 inches deep. Count earthworms. Record the number. (Chemical soil often has 0–2. Healthy soil has 10+.)
- [ ] 🟢 **Water absorption test**: Pour 1 inch of water on the soil surface. Time how long it takes to absorb. (Chemical soil: slow, sits on top. Healthy soil: absorbs in seconds.)
- [ ] 🟢 **Smell test**: Grab a handful of soil. Smell it. Score 1–5 (1 = chemical/no smell, 5 = rich earthy smell).
- [ ] 🟢 **Take photos**: Photograph the soil surface, the dug area, and the overall plot. Date stamp them.

**Why document?** In 3–6 months, you'll repeat these tests. The improvement will be visible and measurable. This is your proof.

### Day 10–11: Stop Chemical Inputs

- [ ] 🟢 **Stop all chemical fertilizers** on the test plot immediately (urea, TSP, MOP, DAP)
- [ ] 🟢 **Stop all chemical pesticides** on the test plot
- [ ] 🟢 **Stop all chemical herbicides** on the test plot
- [ ] 🟢 If there's standing crop with chemicals already applied, wait for harvest, then start ZBNF on the next crop

**🔴 Do NOT do this on your entire farm yet.** Only the test plot. Your other plots continue as before — this is your insurance.

### Day 12–14: Learn the Formulations

- [ ] 🟢 Read the Jeevamrutha recipe thoroughly (see `/docs/research-and-findings/zbnf-practical-guide` Section 2.1)
- [ ] 🟢 Read the Beejamrutha recipe (Section 2.2)
- [ ] 🟢 Read the Neemastra recipe (Section 2.4)
- [ ] 🟢 Watch 2–3 YouTube videos of actual preparation (search "Jeevamrutha preparation")
- [ ] 🟡 If possible, visit a nearby farmer who already practices ZBNF or organic farming
- [ ] 🟢 Plan your first planting: Choose 2–3 crops that grow well together (see `/docs/crop-selection-and-layering/`)

## Troubleshooting

### "I can't find a desi cow anywhere near me"

- Try livestock markets (হাট) in your upazila — ask specifically for desi breed
- Contact the local Livestock Department office — they track indigenous cattle populations
- In worst case, use **goat dung + goat urine** as a substitute. Not as effective but better than nothing.
- Join a farmer group and collectively purchase a desi cow (Red Chittagong calves are affordable)

### "The 200L drum is too expensive"

- Use a **large plastic bucket** (50L) and make quarter-batches
- Check construction sites for discarded food-grade drums
- Ask local restaurants — they often discard large oil drums

### "I don't know which crops to plant"

- **Safest beginner combo**: Tomato + onion + coriander (leafy) + marigold (pest repellent)
- **For rice farmers**: Start ZBNF on your vegetable plot first, not rice. Vegetables show results faster.
- See Phase 2 and `/docs/crop-selection-and-layering/bangladesh-adapted-combinations` for detailed crop combinations

### "My family thinks this is crazy"

- Don't argue. Just do it on the small test plot quietly.
- After one season, the results (lower cost, healthier soil, comparable yield) will speak louder than words.
- Connect with other ZBNF farmers for moral support (see Tool H — Community Network).
